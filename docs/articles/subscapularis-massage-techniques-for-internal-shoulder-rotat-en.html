How to Massage Your Subscapularis and Finally Move Your Shoulder Like You Mean It

If reaching behind your back to tuck in a shirt or fasten a seatbelt has started to feel like a small act of courage, your subscapularis muscle might be screaming for some attention. This deep muscle sits right against the front of your shoulder blade, and when it gets tight and unhappy, internal shoulder rotation becomes stiff and sluggish. The good news is that with the right massage approach, you can start working through that stiffness yourself at home.

Understanding Where the Subscapularis Actually Is

Most people have never heard of the subscapularis, and honestly that makes sense because it hides. It lives on the underside of your shoulder blade, tucked between the blade itself and your ribcage. Its main job is to rotate your arm inward and help keep the ball of your shoulder joint stable. Because of where it sits, you cannot massage it directly like you would a forearm or a calf. Instead, you access it by reaching into the armpit area and pressing along the front edge of the shoulder blade. Sit comfortably with your arm relaxed at your side, reach across your body with the opposite hand, tuck your fingers gently into the armpit, and feel for the ridge of muscle along the inner edge of the shoulder blade. That firm wall of tissue is what you are working with.

The Core Techniques Worth Practicing

Start with gentle sustained pressure. Once your fingertips are positioned on that subscapularis edge, simply hold steady pressure for ten to fifteen seconds, breathe slowly, and let the tissue soften slightly before moving on. This is not about forcing anything. Next, try small circular motions along that inner blade edge, working from top to bottom in a slow rhythm. You will likely find one or two spots that feel noticeably denser or more tender than the rest. Spend a little extra time on those areas with gentle, patient pressure rather than aggressive digging. Another useful technique is a stripping motion where you slowly drag your fingertips from the top of the accessible area downward toward the lower tip of the shoulder blade. This motion works well after the tissue has already been warmed up from a few minutes of the circular work. Keep your shoulder as relaxed as possible throughout. If you tense up, the muscle braces and you lose all the benefit.
