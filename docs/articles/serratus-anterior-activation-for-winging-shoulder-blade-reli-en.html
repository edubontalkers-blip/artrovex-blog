Stop Ignoring Your Winging Shoulder Blade — Here's How Serratus Anterior Work Can Change Everything

If you've ever caught a glimpse of your shoulder blade sticking out like a little wing in the mirror and thought 'that doesn't look right,' you're not alone. A weak or underactive serratus anterior is way more common than people realize, especially if you sit at a desk all day or skip pushing exercises in your workouts. The good news? You can absolutely start working on it today with some focused, intentional movement.

What Is the Serratus Anterior and Why Does It Matter?

The serratus anterior is a fan-shaped muscle that runs along the side of your ribcage, tucking under your shoulder blade and anchoring it flat against your back. When it's doing its job well, your shoulder blade sits flush and moves smoothly every time you reach, press, or lift. When it's not firing properly, the inner edge of your shoulder blade starts to pop off your ribcage — that classic 'winging' look. Beyond aesthetics, a poorly activated serratus anterior tends to throw off your whole shoulder mechanics. Your rotator cuff ends up picking up extra slack, your posture rounds forward, and simple things like reaching overhead or doing a push-up start to feel off. Getting this muscle switched back on is one of the most satisfying things you can do for your upper body function.

The Best Exercises to Wake Up Your Serratus Anterior

The key with serratus anterior training is learning to feel it first, then load it. Start with wall slides — stand facing a wall, forearms flat against it, and slowly slide your arms upward while actively pushing your shoulder blades apart and forward. You're not just moving your arms; you're actively protracting your scapulae the whole way up. Do this slowly, maybe 3 sets of 10, and really pay attention to keeping that blade glued down. Next, try the serratus punch. Lie on your back holding a light weight straight up toward the ceiling, arm locked out, and then reach that weight a few extra inches higher by letting your shoulder blade lift off the floor. Lower it back down. That tiny movement is pure serratus gold. Do 3 sets of 15 reps per side. Once you've got the feel for it, add bear crawl holds — get on all fours, hover your knees an inch off the ground, and hold for 20 to 30 seconds. Push the floor away from you the whole time. This one builds serious serratus endurance and you'll feel it working almost immediately.

Building a Pre-Exercise Routine That Actually Sticks

The biggest mistake people make with serratus work is doing it sporadically and then wondering why nothing changes. What actually works is making it a consistent part of your warm-up or cool-down a few times a week. Start with 5 minutes of thoracic spine mobility — a few cat-cow rounds, maybe some open-book rotations on your side. This gets your upper back moving and sets up better scapular movement from the start. Then move into your serratus exercises with intention, not speed.
